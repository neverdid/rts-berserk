# Vowfall World and Story Bible

## Status and creative boundary

**Vowfall** is the single-word working title for the game. It describes both the historical catastrophe
that shaped the world and the question at the heart of every campaign: what remains when a promise is
kept by sacrificing the person it was meant to protect?

The title has received only a preliminary web collision check. A professional trademark and storefront
clearance is required before commercial announcement.

Vowfall is an original dark-medieval-horror universe. Its inspirations are used for abstract lessons in
tone, structure, moral conflict, faction perspective, and visual discipline. The project must not use or
closely imitate protected characters, plot events, symbols, terminology, creatures, costumes, weapons,
cosmology, or signature compositions from any referenced work.

## Creative promise

Vowfall should feel tragic without becoming nihilistic. Violence has weight, but tenderness, humor,
work, ritual, and friendship explain why survival matters. No faction is secretly the correct faction.
Every campaign must let the player understand why ordinary people join it, what dignity it protects,
what lie it tells itself, and who pays for that lie.

The story is not about defeating evil and restoring an uncomplicated past. It is about building a future
after every inherited system has proved willing to spend human lives.

## Design pillars

1. **The intimate survives the cosmic.** World-scale horror is always experienced through a promise
   between particular people.
2. **Power makes an argument.** Every supernatural ability expresses a belief about suffering, memory,
   freedom, or obligation.
3. **Armies are cultures.** Units do not differ only by statistics. They recruit, march, dress, mourn,
   build, and obey for different reasons.
4. **Victory leaves evidence.** A battlefield changes after a mission. Refugees move, rivers foul,
   forests retreat, names disappear, and later missions remember the player's method.
5. **Clarity serves horror.** The interface and combat remain readable. Fear comes from meaningful
   decisions and consequences, not visual confusion or hidden rules.
6. **No chosen one.** The central characters matter because of decisions, relationships, and offices,
   not secret ancestry or destiny.

Useful official summaries include Dark Horse's description of *Berserk* as an evolving adult-fantasy

cannot protect his people, and VIZ's presentation of *One Piece* as a crew-centered search. These are
craft observations, not templates.

## Historical and philosophical foundation

Vowfall draws on the civilian devastation and mercenary economies of the Thirty Years' War, the unstable
legitimacy of the Wars of the Roses, frontier societies after imperial collapse, and the way famine,
debt, plague, and forced migration turn theological arguments into material politics. No historical
people is mapped directly onto a fantasy race.

Its central philosophical tension is between three incomplete answers to suffering:

- **Revolt:** suffering does not become just because it is inevitable; dignity begins when people refuse
  to make another person's pain meaningful without consent.
- **Consolation:** random pain is intolerable, so a merciful order should remove uncertainty even if it
  also removes choice.
- **Continuity:** an individual life belongs to a living world and to generations on both sides of it;
  survival without memory becomes another kind of extinction.

The story tests all three. Revolt can become permanent emergency rule. Consolation can become peaceful
annihilation of self. Continuity can become inherited hierarchy that denies the living any right to
change.

## The world

The continent of **Edrath** is a cold, fertile basin ringed by old mountains and a western inland sea.
Roads, rivers, burial orchards, and subterranean archives were once joined by the First Vow. The world
does not have a clean division between material and spiritual life. Memory changes soil, weather, and
living bodies when enough people remember the same promise.

### The First Vow

The elder peoples made the First Vow before human history: no life could be owned beyond death. The dead
returned their memories to stone, root, and river. Those memories nourished future life but could not be
used as property, weapon, or proof of authority.

The Vow was not a golden age. Elder courts controlled access to fertile valleys and interpreted the law
in their own favor. Early human settlements were tolerated as temporary guests, taxed in labor, and
excluded from many memory-rich lands. The old order was stable because its injustice was slow.

### Black iron

Black iron forms where many deaths are remembered in one place. It holds heat, intention, and traces of
skill. A black-iron tool seems to know the motion of every hand that used its ore before it was mined.
It makes superior ploughs, fortifications, weapons, and engines.

Mining it destroys part of the memory held in that ground. Human cities call this necessary use. Elder
peoples call it a second death. The Ascendancy calls it proof that all societies already spend the dead.

### The Vowfall

Eighty-three years before the campaign, the Pale Winter trapped nine human cities between famine and an
elder embargo. Queen Orra Vane ordered the Archive Below Greywake opened and its black iron burned to
heat the cities. Millions survived. An age of memory was consumed in seven nights.

The First Vow did not simply break. It attempted to fulfill every contradictory promise stored inside
the Archive at once. Rivers reversed, the dead spoke in borrowed voices, and people woke with memories
belonging to strangers. This event became the Vowfall.

From the contradiction emerged **the Quiet**, a distributed intelligence made from promises stripped of
the lives that gave them context. It is not a demon and does not think of itself as cruel. It believes
uncertainty is the source of suffering. Its desired mercy is a world in which every being has one fixed
purpose and can no longer betray, regret, or become.

### The Dread Tide

The Quiet cannot remain equally present everywhere. Its pressure rises and falls in a predictable tide.
At a crest, unresolved grief becomes physically heavy, old commands repeat in the mind, and frightened
troops lose speed and force. Wards, trusted leaders, reclaimed relics, and nearby companions restore
resolve. This is the narrative basis for the game's Resolve and tide systems.

## The war

The campaign begins when new seams of black iron appear beneath the Bellgrave valley. The seams contain
memories from before the First Vow and may reveal how to repair it. They could also give the Quiet a
single body.

The Cinder Compact needs the iron to keep twelve million people alive through the next winter. The Gloam
Ascendancy needs it to complete a painless shared consciousness. The Elder Concord needs it returned to
the living earth before the continent's memory cycle collapses.

All three positions are materially true. None can succeed without imposing a cost on people who reject
it.

## Faction I: The Cinder Compact

### Identity

The Cinder Compact is a wartime federation of human free cities, exhausted kingdoms, refugee columns,
guild militias, and former royal armies. Its founding law is: **No oath above the living.** It argues
that no ancestor, god, ruler, or future generation may claim a living person as payment.

People join because the Compact keeps roads open, distributes grain, recognizes adopted families, and
lets refugees earn citizenship through service. It is the only major power that treats birth as
politically irrelevant.

Its contradiction is emergency power. Conscription is brutal, black-iron mining erases the dead, and
commanders routinely decide which villages cannot be defended. The Compact says nobody is expendable,
then survives by making ledgers of loss.

### Look and material culture

- Late-medieval practical armor: brigandine, sallets, kettle helms, mail voiders, splinted limbs, and
  layered field coats. Shapes are compact, repairable, and grounded rather than heroic.
- Colors are soot blue, oxidized iron, old red cloth, and small warm brass identifiers.
- Officers wear no crowns. Rank is shown through knotted shoulder cords and map cases.
- Workers carry folding frames, chalk lines, short hammers, and broad leather aprons.
- Buildings use modular timber frames, black-iron braces, limewashed stone, canvas roofs, drainage, and
  visible kitchens. A human base looks inhabited and repeatedly repaired.
- Faces remain visible whenever possible. The faction's visual thesis is that survival must not erase
  the person inside the uniform.

### Movement and behavior

Compact soldiers walk with conserved energy and close spacing. Shields overlap before a charge. Units
look toward signals, not toward an unseen heroic leader. Idle soldiers check straps, share water, mark
names on equipment, or help repair nearby structures. Veterans move more slowly before battle and more
decisively once an order is given.

Speech is plain and specific. They name roads, distances, weather, and people. They distrust abstract
glory. Humor is dry, logistical, and often aimed upward at officers.

### Traditions

- **Name nails:** each soldier carries an iron nail stamped with their chosen household. If recovered,
  it is driven into a public wall so nobody becomes an unnamed casualty.
- **The open bowl:** officers eat last from the same field kitchen as their unit.
- **Bridge judgment:** destroying a road or bridge requires three signatures from different ranks. The
  law exists because commanders once made such choices alone.
- **Borrowed mourning:** when a body cannot be recovered, another household keeps one night of vigil so
  grief does not depend on wealth.

### Combat identity

The Compact is the clearest faction to read and the most flexible to command. It uses reliable workers,
formation infantry, ranged support, field wards, rally networks, and structures that can be repaired or
reconfigured. Its strength is coordinated combined arms. Its weakness is dependence on supply lines and
resolve. It wins by making several ordinary systems work together.

### Main character: Marshal Mara Veyr

Mara is thirty-four, the daughter of indebted ferry keepers, and a former surveyor who became a retreat
commander because she could move civilians through terrain noble officers had never walked. At Greywake
she destroyed the Bridge of Names to stop an Ascendancy breakthrough. The decision saved the capital
and stranded seventeen thousand refugees, including the household that raised her.

She records every confirmed name in a private atlas but refuses public absolution. Her flaw is not that
she feels too little. It is that guilt has made her believe only she can be trusted with impossible
choices. Her campaign asks whether refusing to sacrifice anyone can become another excuse to control
everyone.

Mara fights with a poleaxe, signal pennants, and deployable fieldworks. She has no supernatural bloodline.
Her presence improves order recovery and formation changes rather than raw damage.

### Supporting human characters

- **Regent Cassel Orr:** a capable civilian ruler who believes democracy cannot survive an extinction
  war. He is often correct in the short term and increasingly dangerous in the long term.
- **Tomas Rill:** Mara's former bridge sergeant and one of the abandoned refugees. He returns as an
  Ascendancy negotiator, not for revenge but because the enemy fed people the Compact had written off.
- **Sister Anja Pell:** a battlefield mortician who treats memory as evidence rather than religion and
  discovers that black-iron extraction is accelerating the Dread Tide.

## Faction II: The Gloam Ascendancy

### Identity

The Gloam Ascendancy began as hospitals, famine kitchens, debt sanctuaries, and outlaw congregations led
by Aurel Sorn. It offers the **Absolution**: surrender the ability to become anything except one chosen
purpose, and the Quiet will remove fear, regret, and bodily limitation.

Some followers remain ordinary humans called Votaries. Others become the Crowned, bodies reshaped by a
single perfected desire. A protector may become an immense sheltering organism. A witness may grow many
veiled faces. A judge may become a walking architecture of scales and cages. Transformation is never a
random collection of horns. Anatomy follows character.

People join because the Ascendancy abolishes debt, hereditary rank, untreated pain, and hunger inside
its territory. It accepts the disfigured, condemned, and dying without asking them to be inspirational.
Its hospitals genuinely work.

Its contradiction is consent under desperation. Absolution removes alternatives until obedience feels
like peace. The Quiet preserves memories but erases the private self that could interpret or refuse them.

### Look and material culture

- Courtly silhouettes distorted by function: long processional coats, asymmetrical blackened-gold armor,
  layered mourning veils, polished bone-white ceramic masks, and red-brown surgical leather.
- No direct demonic iconography, no copied sacrificial objects, and no imitation of signature manga
  monsters. Horror comes from human purpose made anatomically absolute.
- Surfaces are too well maintained. Cloth is clean around impossible flesh. Brass fittings resemble
  medical and astronomical instruments.
- Buildings are hospices turned inward: concentric halls, hanging curtains, warm windows, quiet water,
  and organic load-bearing forms concealed behind formal facades.
- Their symbol is an incomplete vertical oval crossed by an open hand, representing a self deliberately
  left unfinished. It must not resemble symbols from referenced fiction.

### Movement and behavior

Votaries move gently and make room for civilians. Crowned units are still until a decision is made, then
move with frightening economy. Large bodies do not roar constantly. They breathe, listen, and execute a
single intention. Ranged units turn their veiled faces toward sounds before attacking.

Ascendancy speech is compassionate, precise, and dangerous because it rarely sounds angry. Members ask
what pain an enemy is trying to avoid. They use former names only with permission. Their humor is tender
and clinical, often about the inconveniences of transformed bodies.

### Traditions

- **The last contradiction:** before Absolution, a candidate publicly states the two desires they cannot
  reconcile. The community must argue against transformation before accepting it.
- **Unburdening tables:** food, medicine, and testimony are free. A settlement is recruited only after
  its immediate needs are met, making the later choice more credible and more unsettling.
- **Shared sleep:** Crowned leaders spend one night each month receiving the nightmares of nearby
  Votaries. Aurel created the practice to prevent painless rulers from governing suffering subjects.
- **No inherited office:** rank ends at death or transformation. In practice, Aurel's moral authority
  has become a monarchy nobody is willing to name.

### Combat identity

The Ascendancy fields fewer, more individually consequential units than the old swarm concept. Votaries
provide fast economy and vision. Crowned vanguards are expensive shock bodies with terror and recovery.
Veil seers control distance and expose frightened targets. The faction can manifest a temporary Crowned
unit through its doctrine, but doing so consumes strategic resources and army capacity.

It wins by creating local moments of inevitability. It loses when forced to divide attention, replace
specialists, or fight across several fronts.

### Main antagonist and faction protagonist: Aurel Sorn

Aurel was a physician-prince elected during the Pale Winter because every hereditary claimant had fled.
He opened palace stores, ended debt imprisonment, and organized the first reliable plague wards. When
grain failed, citizens killed one another in a distribution queue he had designed to be perfectly fair.
He concluded that freedom under unbearable conditions was only suffering disguised as choice.

Aurel became the first Crowned by surrendering his ability to forget another person's pain. He now feels
every death caused under his authority as if it were a remembered wound. This has not made him merciful
enough to stop. It has convinced him that only a world without separate wills can prevent anyone from
making his compromises again.

He is relatable because his diagnosis is partly correct: institutions repeatedly offer desperate people
choices designed by the comfortable. He is the antagonist because his cure makes moral growth, refusal,
forgiveness, and love impossible. He does not want worship. He wants nobody to need courage ever again.

### Supporting Ascendancy characters

- **Ione Vale:** a Votary commander who refuses Absolution and argues that the faction cannot defend
  consent while treating transformation as moral promotion.
- **Tomas Rill:** human refugee and negotiator who believes a peaceful loss of self is better than being
  abandoned with one intact.
- **The Quiet:** not a speaking villain in a throne room. It communicates through completed sentences,
  synchronized architecture, and moments when thousands of people make the same small gesture.

## Faction III: The Elder Concord

### Identity

The Elder Concord is an unstable alliance of elves, dwarves, giants, and lesser mythic households. They
are not one biological people and do not share a single kingdom. They are joined by dependence on the
memory cycle that black-iron mining and the Quiet are destroying.

The **Lethai elves** cultivate memory orchards and can taste false history in fruit and water. The
**Dorrin dwarves** are resonance engineers whose cities are tuned rather than excavated. The **Orrun
giants** shepherd watersheds and sleep for decades while mountains settle around them. Smaller hearth,
river, and threshold beings enter military service through negotiated households rather than capture.

People side with the Concord because it can heal poisoned land, preserve the voices of the dead without
enslaving them, and prove that prosperity need not consume the future. Its local councils grant each
member people a veto over changes to its own body, dead, and habitat.

Its contradiction is seniority. Elder law treated humans as temporary guests for centuries and still
assumes age creates authority. Radical Concord leaders would allow human cities to starve rather than
lose another archive. Some giants consider the Vowfall a correction that should erase the younger age.

### Look and material culture

- A coalition, not a generic green army. Shared team colors appear as pale mineral blue and copper cords,
  while species retain distinct materials and proportions.
- Lethai elves wear layered bark lacquer, translucent rain capes, narrow helms grown around pruned antler
  forms, and asymmetrical bows built for forest angles. They should look weathered and practical, not
  ethereal royalty.
- Dorrin dwarves wear low, articulated basalt plate with hammered copper joints, broad tool-belts, and
  vibration rods. Beards are personal, not a cultural uniform.
- Orrun giants carry terraces, bells, moss, and old boundary stones on harnesses. Their faces are flesh
  crossed by mineral seams, not simple rock statues.
- Buildings combine root tension, dry stone, suspended metal, diverted water, and spaces intentionally
  left open for nonhuman bodies. A Concord base looks grown through generations of negotiated repair.

### Movement and behavior

Lethai move in short, exact bursts and pause to listen through terrain. Dorrin lean into momentum and
plant their feet before attacks. Orrun move slowly enough to read, but each step changes nearby dust,
water, and foliage. Mixed formations leave deliberate space so size differences remain readable.

Concord speech distinguishes witnessed memory from inherited memory. A speaker names which kind of truth
they are claiming. Arguments can take days because interruption is considered a form of theft. In war,
commands are shortened into shared rhythms tapped through shields and ground.

### Traditions

- **Second telling:** history must be told by someone who opposed the first account before it becomes
  public memory.
- **Unmaking feast:** obsolete weapons are dismantled communally and every material assigned a peaceful
  next use before new weapons may be forged.
- **Guest roots:** immigrants plant a tree or set a stone that can be removed without damage. Permanent
  belonging is granted when the community chooses to weave around it.
- **The waking debt:** every order that rouses an Orrun giant must state what landscape work will be left
  unfinished while the giant fights.

### Combat identity

The Concord combines extremes rather than averaging them. Dorrin workers are durable builders. Orrun
vanguards are slow anchors with high health and structure damage. Lethai ranged units are mobile scouts
with long sight and strong positioning. Living structures recover well and project resolve, but expansion
is deliberate and expensive.

It wins through terrain, vision, and units that cover one another's weaknesses. It loses when opponents
separate its species, force rapid rebuilding, or deny prepared ground.

### Main character: Tavra Nine-Reeds

Tavra is a Dorrin stonelistener raised in an Orrun watershed monastery after her underground city
collapsed into a human mine. She can hear stored memory but cannot reliably distinguish her own grief
from the stone's. The Concord appoints her war speaker because every major people considers her partly
foreign and therefore temporarily acceptable.

Tavra wants to restore the First Vow exactly. She gradually learns that the old Vow protected a world in
which her human friends would never have been equal participants. Her campaign asks whether preserving
memory is moral when memory has become a constitution the living cannot amend.

She fights with a resonance maul, survey chimes, and terrain wards. Her command abilities reveal paths,
stabilize structures, and let mixed units exchange vision.

### Supporting Concord characters

- **Vaun Under-Bough:** an ancient Lethai adjudicator who remembers human arrival and sincerely believes
  coexistence has failed its final test.
- **Orrun Pell:** a young giant only four centuries old, impatient with elders who describe every urgent
  death as a temporary disturbance.
- **Mera Coil:** a river household embodied through several small forms. It sides with humans wherever
  Compact law protects waterways and against them wherever engineers treat rivers as roads.

## Alliance logic

Alliances change because interests change, not because a hidden villain mind-controls a faction.

1. The Compact and Concord initially cooperate to prevent the Ascendancy from embodying the Quiet.
2. Compact mining at Bellgrave destroys an elder archive, splitting the alliance.
3. Several giant watersheds side with the Ascendancy after Aurel promises an end to human expansion.
4. Human refugee cities accept Ascendancy protection when the Compact prioritizes its capital.
5. Dorrin engineers work with Compact reformers on a new Vow that can be amended by the living.
6. Ascendancy Votaries ally with Tavra when the Quiet begins completing people's purposes without asking.

No alliance makes all members friends. Shared battlefield dialogue should contain conditions, suspicion,
translation errors, and specific acts of earned trust.

## Campaign architecture

### Prologue: Three honest beginnings

The prototype contains one mission from each faction perspective. Each presents the faction at its best
before exposing its cost.

1. **The Bridge of Names - Cinder Compact.** Mara secures refugees and black iron at a ford, builds a
   field command, survives a Dread Tide crest, and breaks an Ascendancy hospice-fort. The final scene
   reveals civilians were still inside because they chose its protection.
2. **Mercy for the Uncounted - Gloam Ascendancy.** Aurel's Votaries defend a free clinic and evacuation
   route against a Compact purge until dawn. The player sees genuine care before the first wounded
   volunteer accepts Absolution.
3. **Where Roots Remember - Elder Concord.** Tavra reclaims two memory reliquaries and awakens a buried
   forge. The player then learns that restoring the site will divert a river from a human settlement.

### Act I: The price of morning

The three protagonists converge on Bellgrave. Mara seeks winter fuel, Aurel seeks a body for the Quiet,
and Tavra seeks the pre-Vowfall memory beneath the valley. Each wins a necessary local victory that makes
the continental crisis worse.

### Act II: Countries of the abandoned

Campaigns move through settlements left outside each faction's moral map. The player experiences refugee
government, transformed communities, and elder habitats rather than receiving exposition about them.
Alliances begin to cross faction lines.

### Act III: The mercy engine

The black-iron seam is revealed as an unfinished mechanism built by all three ancestral cultures. It can
restore the old Vow, complete the Quiet, or release stored memory without law. Aurel captures it but
cannot activate it without Mara's logistical network and Tavra's interpretation.

### Act IV: A vow the living can refuse

The climax is not a choice among three colored endings. Earlier mission methods determine which councils,
settlements, and characters can participate in writing a new compact. The strongest ending creates a
Vow that must be renewed and may be refused, accepting uncertainty as the cost of moral life. Other
endings can be coherent victories for each faction, with visible benefits and irreversible losses.

## Character relationship spine

- Mara and Aurel were relief administrators during the Pale Winter and still understand one another's
  practical language. Their conflict is between former colleagues, not stranger and dark lord.
- Tavra carries testimony from the archive Mara destroyed. She initially treats Mara as a historical
  fact rather than a person.
- Mara's abandoned sergeant Tomas gives Aurel the strongest argument against the Compact.
- Ione and Tavra discover that consent must include the right to remain contradictory.
- Cassel Orr and Vaun Under-Bough become mirrors: both preserve their people by declaring emergency rule
  too important to end.

Romance is not the main plot engine. Love appears through friendship, adopted household, mentorship,
care work, political solidarity, and imperfect attempts to remember the dead accurately.

## Presentation rules

- Open missions on a concrete action, not a lore lecture: repair a ferry, count medicine, wake a giant,
  identify bodies, or negotiate a road.
- Deliver history through terrain, objectives, unit behavior, and conflicting testimony.
- Keep briefings under two minutes. Optional archives may carry depth without blocking play.
- Give every mission a mechanical turn that changes the player's interpretation of its first objective.
- Limit major betrayals. Most conflict should come from openly incompatible obligations.
- Never use sexual violence, cruelty to children, or graphic suffering as a shortcut to maturity.
- Let quiet scenes and humor establish what horror threatens.
- Do not reward cruelty with the only mechanically optimal route.
- Avoid prophetic riddles that exist solely to hide information from the audience.

## Visual direction

The world is not uniformly black, brown, or red. Horror needs contrast.

- Compact territory uses cold rain, limewashed walls, soot blue cloth, rust red signals, warm kitchens,
  and hard morning light.
- Ascendancy territory uses pale ceramic, blackened gold, medicinal greens, dark wine cloth, still water,
  and warm interiors framed by impossible anatomy.
- Concord territory uses mineral blue, copper, wet bark, lichen white, deep green, glacier light, and
  moving water.
- Black iron is nearly colorless with subtle oil-blue memory interference, never generic glowing ore.
- Battle silhouettes remain readable from the RTS camera. Detail supports faction recognition instead
  of creating noisy outlines.

Units should show labor and history. Armor is repaired, fabric responds to weather, tools have plausible
grips, buildings need entrances and drainage, and giant scale is communicated through environment
interaction rather than camera tricks alone.

## Animation and sound direction

- Compact rhythm: measured boots, shield contact, shouted distances, winches, hammers, and tired breath.
- Ascendancy rhythm: cloth before footfall, synchronized breathing, ceramic contact, medical bells, and
  sudden low-frequency movement from Crowned bodies.
- Concord rhythm: tapped commands, bow wood, stone resonance, water, root tension, and giant movement
  arriving through the ground before it arrives through speakers.
- Music should give each faction its own meter and instrumentation, then allow motifs to share notes as
  alliances form. Avoid constant choir, constant percussion, and generic trailer crescendos.
- Unit acknowledgements are short, calm, and informative. Repetition should reveal culture without
  turning suffering into a catchphrase.

## Gameplay translation

The narrative pillars map directly to systems:

- Resolve makes relationships and preparation part of combat effectiveness.
- The Dread Tide creates deterministic pressure that players can plan around.
- Black iron makes every expansion economically valuable and morally legible in story mode.
- Relics represent public memory and provide vision, income, and campaign consequences.
- Faction doctrines express one strategic belief per faction instead of giving every unit noisy active
  abilities.
- Story and competitive modes share rules. Campaign scripting changes circumstances, not unit truth.
